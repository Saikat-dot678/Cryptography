# 🔐 Section 1.2 — The Setting of Private-Key Encryption

Before defining security, we must define:

**What exactly is an encryption scheme?** 🤔

Most beginners say:
> "Encryption turns plaintext into ciphertext." ❌

This is too vague.
Cryptography requires a precise mathematical model. 📐

---

## 🎭 The Basic Scenario

There are three parties:

* **Alice 👩:** Sender
* **Bob 👨:** Receiver
* **Eve 🦹‍♀️:** Adversary

Alice wants to send a message:
$m$
to Bob.

The communication channel is public. 🌐
Meaning:
`Alice ---> Internet ---> Bob`

Eve can observe everything sent over the channel. 👀
Therefore Alice cannot simply send:
$m$
directly.

Instead she encrypts it. 🛡️

---

## 🧩 What Is an Encryption Scheme?

A private-key encryption scheme consists of three algorithms.
This is the first formal cryptographic syntax you'll encounter. 📜

### 1. Key Generation Algorithm 🔑
Denoted:
`Gen`
Generates a secret key.

$k \leftarrow \text{Gen}()$

Output:
$k$

Example:
$k = 110101101011...$ (256 random bits).

**Important:** ⚠️
The key must be random. 🎲
Not: `password123`
Not: `mybirthday`
Randomness is crucial.

### 2. Encryption Algorithm 🔒
Denoted:
`Enc`
Input:
$k, m$
Output:
$c$

Formally:
$c \leftarrow \text{Enc}(k, m)$

Example:
$m = \text{"HELLO"}$
becomes
$c = \text{8F7A19...}$

### 3. Decryption Algorithm 🔓
Denoted:
`Dec`
Input:
$k, c$
Output:
$m$

Formally:
$m \leftarrow \text{Dec}(k, c)$

Thus:
* **Alice:** $c \leftarrow \text{Enc}(k, m)$
* **Bob:** $m \leftarrow \text{Dec}(k, c)$

---

## ✅ Correctness

The first requirement is:

**Correctness Property** 🎯
When Alice encrypts and Bob decrypts:
Bob must recover the original message.

Formally:
$\text{Dec}(k, \text{Enc}(k, m)) = m$
for every valid message.

This may look trivial.
But it is a fundamental requirement.
A scheme that sometimes decrypts incorrectly is useless. 🗑️

---

## 🖼️ Visualizing the System

```text
            Key k
              |
              v

Message m --> Enc --> Ciphertext c

Ciphertext c --> Dec --> Message m

              ^
              |
            Key k

```

**Notice:** 💡
The same key is used for encryption and decryption.
Therefore:
**Private-Key Encryption = Symmetric Encryption** 🤝
These terms mean the same thing.

---

## 🚧 Why Is Key Distribution Difficult?

Now we encounter the first major problem.
Suppose Alice and Bob want to communicate.
They need the same secret key:
$k$

How do they obtain it? 🤔

* **Option 1:** Alice emails it. (Bad. Eve reads it.) 🚫
* **Option 2:** Alice sends it through the same network. (Bad. Eve reads it.) 🚫
* **Option 3:** Meet physically. (Works. But not scalable.) ✅

Imagine:
1 billion users meeting physically. 🌍
Impossible.

This problem is called:
**The Key Distribution Problem** 📬
Historically this was one of the biggest motivations behind public-key cryptography.
We'll revisit this in Chapter 9.

---

## ⚔️ Attack Scenarios

Now comes an important conceptual shift. 🧠
Historically people asked:
*How do we encrypt?*

Modern cryptography asks:
*Against what attack are we securing?* 🛡️

The attack model matters.

### Ciphertext-Only Attack 🕵️

Attacker sees:
$c_1, c_2, c_3, \dots$
and tries to learn something.
Example:
$\text{AES}(k, m)$ messages traveling over WiFi.

### Known-Plaintext Attack 🔍

Attacker sees pairs:
$(m, c)$
Example:
Every company email starts: "Dear Sir,"
Attacker knows part of plaintext.

### Chosen-Plaintext Attack (CPA) 🎯

Attacker can request encryptions.
Example:
`Encrypt("AAAA")`
`Encrypt("BBBB")`
`Encrypt("CCCC")`
and observe outputs.
This is much stronger. 💪
Modern schemes are expected to survive this.

---

## 💭 What Does Eve Actually Want?

This sounds obvious.
You may think:
*Eve wants the message.*

Not necessarily. 🙅‍♀️
Sometimes partial information is enough. 🧩

Suppose Alice encrypts:
Salary = $100000 💰

Imagine Eve learns:
Salary > $50000 📈

Even without recovering the full message.
Privacy has already been broken. 💥
This observation changed cryptography forever.

---

## 🚫 Why Recovering the Plaintext Is the Wrong Goal

Consider a scheme where Eve cannot recover:
$m$
but can determine:
$m$ is even
or
$m$ is odd

Would you call that secure?
No. Information leaked. 💧

Suppose:
Medical record encrypted. 🏥
Eve learns:
Patient has cancer
without learning anything else.

The scheme is clearly insecure. 🚨

Therefore:
Modern cryptography does NOT define security as:
*Attacker cannot recover plaintext.*

Instead:
**Attacker cannot learn ANY useful information.** 🧠

This idea eventually becomes:

* Semantic Security
* Indistinguishability
* CPA Security
which we study deeply in Chapter 3. 📚

---

## 📦 Message Space

Another important concept.
Not all strings are necessarily valid messages.

Define:
$\mathcal{M}$
called the message space.

Example:
$\mathcal{M} = \{0,1\}^{128}$ (All 128-bit strings).
Or:
$\mathcal{M} = \text{all ASCII strings}$

The encryption algorithm must operate on messages from this space. 🛤️

---

## 🧳 Ciphertext Space

Similarly define:
$\mathcal{C}$
the ciphertext space.

Example:
AES takes:
128-bit plaintext
and produces:
128-bit ciphertext

Thus:
$\mathcal{M} = \mathcal{C} = \{0,1\}^{128}$
for a single AES block. 🧱

---

## 🎲 Why Randomness Matters

Many beginners think encryption should be deterministic.
Example:
`HELLO` always becomes `8F7A...`

This is dangerous. ⚠️
Suppose Eve observes:
$c_1 = c_2$

Then Eve immediately knows:
$m_1 = m_2$
even without decrypting.

Information leaked. 🚨

Therefore modern encryption usually uses randomness. 🌀
Same plaintext:
`HELLO`
can produce:
$c_1$ today
and
$c_2$ tomorrow.

This concept becomes crucial in Chapter 3 when we study CPA-secure encryption. 📆

## ✅ What you should know before moving on

You should be able to answer:

* **What are the three algorithms?** 🧮
`Gen`, `Enc`, `Dec`
* **What is correctness?** 🎯
$\text{Dec}(k, \text{Enc}(k, m)) = m$
* **What is the key distribution problem?** 📬
How Alice and Bob obtain a shared secret key.
* **Why isn't "cannot recover plaintext" enough?** 🧩
Because leaking any useful information already breaks privacy.
* **Why do modern schemes use randomness?** 🎲
To prevent information leakage from repeated encryptions.
