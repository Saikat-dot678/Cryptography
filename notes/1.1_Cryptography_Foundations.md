# 📖 Chapter 1 — Introduction and Classical Ciphers

## 🤔 1.1 What Is Cryptography?

Most beginners think:
> **Cryptography = Encryption** ❌

This is wrong. Modern cryptography is much broader. The book defines modern cryptography as:
> *The scientific study of techniques for securing digital information, transactions, and distributed computations.* 🔬

### 🎯 Examples:

* **Confidentiality 🤫**
    * Only Alice and Bob can read the message.
    * *Example:* WhatsApp messages, Signal messages.
* **Integrity 🛡️**
    * Nobody can modify the message.
    * *Example:* Transfer $100 becoming Transfer $100000 must be detectable.
* **Authentication 👤**
    * How do we know the sender is really Alice?
* **Non-repudiation 📜**
    * Alice cannot later claim: "I never sent that message."
    * This is the purpose of digital signatures.
* **Secure Computation 🤝**
    * Two parties compute a function jointly without revealing inputs.
    * *Example:* Millionaire Problem (Who is richer without revealing actual wealth?)
    * This eventually leads to **MPC**, **ZK Proofs**, and **FHE**, which are active research fields.

---

## 🏛️ Why Modern Cryptography Exists

Historically cryptography was an art. People designed ciphers. If nobody broke them: Looks secure. 

⚠️ **This is extremely dangerous.**
*Example:* Designer: "I cannot break it." does **NOT** imply "Nobody can break it."

The entire philosophy of modern cryptography is:
> **Security must be mathematically defined and mathematically proven.** 📐

---

## 📡 The Communication Model

The book introduces a simple setting. Alice wants to send a message ($m$) to Bob.

1.  Alice computes: $c = \text{Enc}(k, m)$
2.  Alice sends: ciphertext $c$
3.  Bob computes: $m = \text{Dec}(k, c)$ using the same key.

### 🔑 Symmetric Encryption
Same key used by both.
`Alice ----k---- Bob`
* *Examples:* AES, ChaCha20

## 🦹 The Adversary

Modern cryptography assumes an attacker exists. Traditionally called: **Eve** (Eavesdropper) 🕵️‍♀️

Eve can:
* Listen 🎧
* Record 📼
* Analyze 📊
* Modify 🛠️
communications.

The question is: **What can Eve learn?** This is the core question of cryptography.

---

## 🧠 Kerckhoffs's Principle

One of the most important ideas in the entire field. The book emphasizes this heavily.

> **Principle:** The security of a cryptosystem must depend only on the secrecy of the key, not on secrecy of the algorithm. 🗝️

* **Bad design:** Security by obscurity 🙈
    * *Example:* "Our encryption algorithm is secret." Eventually leaked. System dies.
* **Good design:** AES is public. 🌍
    * Everyone knows exactly how it works. Yet secure. Why? Because only the key is secret.

This principle is the foundation of AES, RSA, Signal, TLS, Bitcoin signatures, and everything modern.

---

## ⚔️ Attack Models

Cryptographers define the attacker's power. This is critical. The book repeatedly stresses:
> **Security = No adversary of specified power achieves a specified break.** 🛑

Three classical attack models:

1.  **Ciphertext-Only Attack (COA) 🕵️**
    * Attacker sees: $c_1, c_2, c_3, \dots$ only.
2.  **Known-Plaintext Attack (KPA) 🔍**
    * Attacker sees: $(m, c)$ pairs.
    * *Example:* "Dear Sir" appears in many encrypted emails.
3.  **Chosen-Plaintext Attack (CPA) 🎯**
    * Attacker chooses messages. Asks encryption oracle: `Encrypt("Hello")`, `Encrypt("Attack")`, `Encrypt("123")` and observes outputs.
    * Modern encryption **must** survive CPA.

## 📏 Why Formal Definitions Matter

Suppose I ask: *What does secure encryption mean?*
Many answers are wrong.

* **Wrong:** Attacker cannot recover plaintext. *(Still leaks information.)*
* **Wrong:** Attacker cannot recover entire plaintext. *(May leak partial information.)*
* **Correct intuition:** Attacker should learn **nothing useful** about the plaintext. ✅

Making this precise takes the next several chapters.

---

## 📐 Three Principles of Modern Cryptography

The authors summarize modern cryptography using three ideas:

* **Principle 1:** Precise definitions. 📝
* **Principle 2:** Precise assumptions. 💭
    * *Example:* "Factoring is hard" must be mathematically defined.
* **Principle 3:** Rigorous proofs. 🧮
    * Prove: "If factoring is hard, then RSA is secure." instead of saying: "Seems secure."

This transformation is why cryptography became a science rather than an art. 🧪
